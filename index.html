Runner's
Knee
Fix

A structured program to rebuild hip and glute strength, reduce knee pain, and get you back running stronger than before.

Hip and Glute Protocol
Strength Exercises

These moves target the glutes and hips, the root cause of most runner's knee cases. Weak glutes force your knee to compensate on every stride.

01

Clamshells

3 x 15 each side Glute Med
▼

How To

  • 1Lie on your side, hips stacked, knees bent at 45 degrees
  • 2Keep feet together and rotate top knee upward like a clamshell opening
  • 3Stop at 45 degrees — do not let your hip roll back
  • 4Slowly lower back down with control
Pro tip: Add a resistance band above the knees once bodyweight feels easy. You should feel a burn in the side of your hip.
02

Glute Bridges

3 x 20 Glute Max
▼

How To

  • 1Lie on back, feet flat on floor, knees bent at 90 degrees
  • 2Drive through heels and squeeze glutes to lift hips
  • 3Hold at top for 2 seconds — body forms a straight line
  • 4Lower slowly, do not let your hips drop quickly
Progress: Move to single-leg glute bridges once comfortable. Place a weight across your hips for added load.
03

Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift

3 x 10 each side Glutes + Hamstrings
▼

How To

  • 1Stand on one leg with a slight bend in the knee
  • 2Hinge at hips, reach hands toward floor as rear leg extends back
  • 3Keep back flat and hips square — do not let hip rotate open
  • 4Squeeze glute to return to standing
Key: Go slow (3 sec down) to maximize glute and hip stability gains. Use a wall for balance if needed early on.
04

Side-Lying Hip Abduction

3 x 15 each side Hip Abductors
▼

How To

  • 1Lie on your side with legs straight and stacked
  • 2Keeping foot flexed, lift top leg 12 to 18 inches
  • 3Do not let the hip roll back — keep it stacked
  • 4Lower with control — no dropping
Level up: Add ankle weights or a resistance band at the ankles for more burn.
05

Step-Ups with Knee Drive

3 x 12 each side Full Chain
▼

How To

  • 1Place one foot on a sturdy box or step (8 to 12 inches)
  • 2Drive through your heel to step up, driving opposite knee up
  • 3Stand tall at the top, then lower the trailing leg slowly
  • 4Keep knee tracking over second toe — do not cave inward
Runner specific: The knee drive mimics running mechanics and directly transfers to your stride.
06

Wall Sit with Hip Squeeze

3 x 45 seconds Quads + VMO
▼

How To

  • 1Back flat against wall, feet shoulder-width apart
  • 2Slide down to 90 degrees at the knee
  • 3Place a ball or rolled towel between knees and squeeze
  • 4Hold, focusing on the inner quad (VMO) near the kneecap
Why it matters: The VMO stabilizes the kneecap. Strengthening it directly reduces runner's knee pain.
07

Lateral Band Walk

3 x 15 steps each way Glute Med + Min
▼

How To

  • 1Place a resistance band just above or below your knees
  • 2Stand in a half-squat with feet hip-width apart and toes forward
  • 3Step sideways, keeping band tension the whole way — do not let feet come together
  • 4Take 15 steps left, then 15 right — that is one set
Form check: Keep your torso upright and avoid shifting your weight side to side. The glutes, not your lower back, should be doing the work.
08

Nordic Hamstring Curl

3 x 6–8 reps Hamstrings (Eccentric)
▼

How To

  • 1Kneel on a pad with feet anchored under a couch or bar
  • 2With hands ready to catch you, lower your torso forward as slowly as possible
  • 3Use your hands to push off the floor, then contract your hamstrings to pull back up
  • 4Aim for a 4 to 6 second lowering phase
Why it's elite: Eccentric hamstring strength is one of the best injury-prevention tools in running. Studies show it reduces hamstring and knee injuries by up to 50%. Start Phase 2 only.
09

Terminal Knee Extension (TKE)

3 x 20 each leg VMO / Quad Control
▼

How To

  • 1Anchor a resistance band to a fixed point at knee height and loop it behind your knee
  • 2Step away to create tension, stand on one leg with knee slightly bent
  • 3Straighten your knee against the band resistance by contracting your quad — focus on feeling the VMO (inner quad) fire
  • 4Slowly return to the start, controlling the band
Physical therapy staple: The TKE is one of the most targeted exercises for patellofemoral pain. It trains the VMO in the exact range where runner's knee hurts most — near full extension.
Mobility and Recovery
Stretching Routine

Do these after every run and strength session. Hold each for the time shown. No bouncing. Consistency here prevents future flare-ups.

01
Standing Quad Stretch
30s x 3 each Quads · Kneecap Tension
▼

How To

  • 1Stand tall, use a wall for balance with one hand
  • 2Pull one foot toward your glute, keeping knees together
  • 3Tilt your pelvis slightly forward for a deeper stretch
  • 4Hold and switch sides
Why it matters: Tight quads are a top cause of patellofemoral pain. This is non-negotiable after every run.
02
Figure-4 Piriformis Stretch
45s x 2 each Glutes · Piriformis · IT Band
▼

How To

  • 1Lie on your back, cross one ankle over the opposite knee
  • 2Pull the lower leg toward your chest
  • 3Hold until you feel a deep stretch in the outer glute
  • 4Keep your head and shoulders flat on the floor
Key: Tight piriformis alters hip mechanics and directly stresses the knee on every stride.
03
Hip Flexor Lunge Stretch
45s x 2 each Hip Flexors · Psoas
▼

How To

  • 1Kneel in a lunge position, back knee on the floor
  • 2Push hips forward until you feel a stretch in the front of the back hip
  • 3Keep core braced and torso tall — do not arch the back
  • 4Hold and switch sides
Why it matters: Tight hip flexors alter your stride and overload the knee. Critical for desk workers.
04
IT Band Cross-Leg Stretch
30s x 3 each IT Band · TFL
▼

How To

  • 1Stand and cross one leg behind the other
  • 2Lean toward the side of the back leg, pushing your hip out
  • 3Feel the stretch along the outer thigh down to the knee
  • 4Use a wall for support if needed
Key: A tight IT band pulls the kneecap laterally — one of the most common runner's knee triggers.
05
Downward Dog Calf Stretch
30s x 2 each Calves · Achilles
▼

How To

  • 1Start in downward dog, hips high, hands and feet on floor
  • 2Press one heel toward the floor while keeping the other slightly raised
  • 3Hold, then alternate — feel the stretch up the calf
  • 4Foam roll calves first for best results
Why it matters: Tight calves change your landing mechanics and increase knee load with every footstrike.
06
Supine Hamstring Stretch
45s x 2 each Hamstrings · Posterior Chain
▼

How To

  • 1Lie on your back and lift one leg, holding behind the knee or calf
  • 2Keep the opposite leg flat on the floor
  • 3Straighten the raised leg as much as comfortable
  • 4Use a strap or towel around the foot if needed
Key: Tight hamstrings pull the pelvis and increase compressive forces at the kneecap.
07
Seated Butterfly Stretch
60s x 1 Hip Adductors · Groin
▼

How To

  • 1Sit with soles of feet together, knees falling out to the sides
  • 2Sit tall — do not round the lower back
  • 3Gently press knees toward the floor with your elbows
  • 4Hold and breathe — let gravity do the work
Why it matters: Tight adductors pull the knee inward — a direct cause of kneecap irritation in runners.
08
Seated Towel Calf Stretch
45s x 2 each Gastrocnemius · Soleus
▼

How To

  • 1Sit on the floor with legs straight out in front
  • 2Loop a towel or strap around the ball of one foot
  • 3Gently pull toes toward you — knee straight for gastroc, slightly bent for soleus
  • 4Hold and switch sides
Two stretches in one: Straight knee = gastrocnemius. Slightly bent knee = soleus. Do both for full calf release.
09
Child's Pose with Side Reach
45s x 2 each side Thoracic · Lats · Glutes
▼

How To

  • 1Start in child's pose — hips back toward heels, arms forward
  • 2Walk both hands to one side and hold
  • 3Feel the lateral stretch through your ribs and lat
  • 4Return to center and repeat on the other side
Why it matters: Thoracic stiffness affects arm swing, which cascades down to altered hip mechanics and knee stress.
10
Ankle Circles & Dorsiflexion
20 circles + 30s hold Ankle Mobility · Knee Alignment
▼

How To

  • 1Seated, lift one foot and make 10 large clockwise circles
  • 2Repeat 10 counter-clockwise
  • 3Then flex the foot strongly toward your shin and hold 30 seconds
  • 4Switch sides
Often missed: Poor ankle dorsiflexion is a hidden driver of runner's knee — it forces the knee to compensate with every footstrike.
Recovery Timeline
3-Phase Plan

Most runners are back to full training in 4 to 8 weeks. Move to the next phase only when the current one feels pain-free.

1
Weeks 1-2 — Phase 1
Calm the Inflammation
Ice 15 to 20 min after activity. Stop running or drop to easy walks only. Do the full stretch routine daily. Begin Exercises 01, 02, and 04 only at low load, high reps, pain-free range only.
2
Weeks 3-5 — Phase 2
Build the Foundation
Add all 6 exercises 3x per week. Reintroduce easy running at 30 to 40% of previous volume on flat surfaces only. No speed work. Focus on cadence, aim for 170 to 180 spm to reduce knee load.
3
Weeks 6-8 — Phase 3
Load and Return
Progress to resistance bands and added weight. Gradually rebuild run volume at +10% per week max. Introduce hills carefully. Maintain strength work 2x per week as maintenance going forward.
